📖 Today's Featured Reading

Big Book — Personal Stories — May 7

Women Suffer, Too

Despite great opportunities, alcohol nearly ended her life. Early member, she spread the word among women in our pioneering period. — Editor's note, Big Book p. 200 (4th ed.)

I went to a meeting to see for myself this group of freaks or bums who had done this thing. To go into a gathering of people was the sort of thing that all my life, from the time I left my private world of books and dreams to meet the real world of people and parties and jobs, had left me feeling an uncomfortable outsider, needing the warming stimulus of drinks to join in.

I went trembling into a house in Brooklyn filled with strangers . . . and I found I had come home at last, to my own kind. There is another meaning for the Hebrew word that in the King James version of the Bible is translated "salvation." It is: "to come home." I had found my salvation. I wasn't alone any more.

— Marty Mann, "Women Suffer, Too," Big Book p. 206. Marty was the first woman to achieve long-term sobriety in AA. Her first meeting was at Bill and Lois Wilson's home at 182 Clinton Street, Brooklyn, on April 11, 1939. She stayed sober until her death in 1980.

🎯 Deep Dive — Salvation as Coming Home

Stop and look at the line again. "There is another meaning for the Hebrew word that in the King James version of the Bible is translated 'salvation.' It is: to come home."

This is doing more theological work than most sermons. The English word salvation has been weighed down by two thousand years of churchy baggage — fire, judgment, who's in and who's out, the sinner and the saved. Marty quietly puts that aside and reaches for the older, plainer image underneath. Salvation isn't a verdict. It's a homecoming. It's the moment the alcoholic — who has been wandering inside their own skull for years, alone in every room they've ever been in — walks into a place and recognizes the people in it as their own.

Think about what Marty was carrying when she walked into 182 Clinton Street. Wealthy upbringing. Boarding schools. A debutante ball. Marriage into a prominent family. A career at Vogue and Tatler. Friends who included Virginia Woolf and Janet Flanner. By every external measure, she had the most enviable life of anyone in the room. And the entire interior architecture of that life was an experience of being an "uncomfortable outsider, needing the warming stimulus of drinks to join in." That's the alcoholic loneliness. It scales with privilege rather than against it.

She walks into a small house in Brooklyn — filled with strangers, in her own words — and the loneliness ends. Not because she now had friends. Friends she'd had. The loneliness ends because she met her own kind. People whose interior lives had the same shape as hers. People who knew, without being told, what the shaking hands at 6 a.m. meant. "I had come home at last."

This is what fellowship is for, and it's why "I'll just get sober on my own" almost never works. The drinking was the symptom. The deeper trouble was that the alcoholic mind had built a private universe of one and forgotten how to leave it. Meetings, sponsors, the quiet phone call after — these aren't optional accessories on the program. They're the homecoming itself. The literal, weekly, in-person experience of not being alone with this anymore.

Where do you come home to? And when's the last time you walked in?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What strikes me about Marty's story, taken whole, is how much courage was required to write it in the first place. The first edition of the Big Book contained one woman's story — Florence R., titled "A Feminine Victory" — and she relapsed and died of her alcoholism before the second edition came out. When the second edition was assembled in 1955, Marty's story took its place. She was, in print, telling the world that women got drunk too. In 1955. Fifteen years before alcoholism would even be officially recognized as a disease by the American Medical Association. Forty years before "high-functioning alcoholic" entered the popular vocabulary.

The phrase she chose for the title — "Women Suffer, Too" — has a quiet defiance in it. The comma is doing work. Too. Also. Don't forget us. The standing assumption in 1955 was that the alcoholic was a man on a barstool. Marty made the case, in the most authoritative book the program owned, that the same disease was running through a different population entirely — and that the program worked for them too.

The line about salvation is the heart of her story, but the structural fact is just as worth noticing: she walked into a room of men in 1939 who were genuinely uncertain whether she belonged there, and she stayed. The fellowship became more than it had been because she did. Whatever room any of us walks into today — meeting, group, gathering of fellow miners — somebody walked in first when it was harder. The seat we sit in was held open by someone who held the door against the wind.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few nuggets pulled from the rest of today's readings, in case you don't get to them yourself:

  • Today's Language of Letting Go (Beattie) names fear as the core of codependency. Not love, not caretaking — fear. The nugget worth carrying: many of us have been afraid for so long we've stopped labeling the feeling — it just feels like normal, while peace and serenity feel uncomfortable. Beattie's move is gentle: thank the old fear for the years of survival service, wave it goodbye, listen to your healthy fears, let the rest go. If anything in that landed, the full reading today is short and worth opening.
  • Today's Father Leo meditation is on success. The whole piece is about taking responsibility — for the failures and for the successes. Most of us are cheerfully fluent in the first column and freeze up at the second. The line worth lifting from his reading today: "This underling is learning how to fly." A perfect summary of what the program quietly does to a person who really works it.
  • Today's 12&12 reading is Step Seven on humility. The nugget Bill leaves at the top of the chapter: "Every newcomer in Alcoholics Anonymous is told, and soon realizes for himself, that his humble admission of powerlessness over alcohol is his first step toward liberation from its paralyzing grip." The whole step rests on a backwards-feeling claim — that the doorway out of being controlled by something is admitting you can't beat it on your own. Still the most useful sentence in the chapter.
  • Today's Walk in Dry Places is on dysfunctional families. The reading takes a steady, slightly contrarian line worth carrying: most of our parents did the best they could, and treating childhood as a permanent handicap is itself a form of staying stuck. The fellowship is full of people who went through the same thing and built lives anyway. Not a denial of the pain — a refusal to let it have the last word.
