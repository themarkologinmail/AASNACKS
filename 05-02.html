📖 Today's Featured Reading

One Day at a Time — May 2

Helping Others (and Counting the Good Cans)

If I can stop one heart from breaking, I shall not live in vain. If I can ease one life the aching, or cool one pain, or help one fainting robin unto his nest again, I shall not live in vain. — Emily Dickinson

Somewhere along the way I found myself to be a caretaker. Injustices, pain, discrimination, bullying — all these things affected me deeply. I carried it too far. It reached a point where I truly believe I began taking better care of others than I did myself. Was this ego? Codependency? Altruism? Or was this a guiltless way I found to deflect my own problems, pain, injustices and needs?

When I was doing my first 4th Step inventory, I learned something very important. As my sponsor read over one "bad thing" I had done after another, she cautioned me to take a broader look at myself. Finally, she made me do my entire inventory over and for every 5th character defect or offense to someone, I was required to write something good about myself.

She explained that an inventory is never meant to be focused on just the bad … but the good also. After all, when a store takes inventory on its products, it counts bent cans of beans as well as the perfect cans of beans, and crushed boxes of cereal as well as the perfect ones.

This helped me to see that my life's purpose was not just to help others but also to nurture me when my heart was breaking, to make my own life good and to have a nest for myself that was safe and serene.

— Adapted from Mari's reflection in One Day at a Time, May 2

🎯 Deep Dive — Why Most People Do the 4th Step Wrong

Mari's sponsor noticed something most sponsors don't catch — and most newcomers don't realize they're doing. The 4th step, as it gets practiced in the rooms, almost universally turns into a one-sided indictment. Page after page of resentments, fears, sex conduct, and harm done. By the end, the person doing the inventory has constructed a tidy case against themselves, signed in their own handwriting.

That's not what the step says. "Searching and fearless moral inventory" is the wording, and an inventory is by definition a count of everything in stock. Bent cans of beans, perfect cans of beans, crushed boxes, intact boxes. If you walked into a Costco and the manager handed you a list of every dented can in the warehouse and called it "inventory," you'd correctly think the manager had a problem. But that's exactly what most of us do to ourselves.

Why? Because shame is sneaky. It hijacks the work that's supposed to free us and turns it into yet another way to reinforce the story we already believe about ourselves. The literature is well aware of this — it's why the 12&12 spends so much time talking about defects as "instincts gone astray," not as proof of moral failure. But most newcomers never get the memo, and most sponsors never push back. So the 4th step becomes the prosecution and Step 5 becomes the sentencing.

If you're working on a 4th step right now — or reviewing an old one — Mari's sponsor offers a quiet correction: for every five "wrongs" you list, write down one good thing about yourself. Not a humblebrag. An actual asset. A real strength. A genuine kindness you've shown. The instinct will be to resist. That resistance is the data. The fact that listing 80 character defects feels normal but listing 16 personal strengths feels uncomfortable tells you exactly which side of the ledger you've been refusing to look at.

Your strengths aren't bragging. They're inventory. Count them.

🤖 Claude's Corner

What I find moving about Mari's story is that the correction came from her sponsor, not the literature. The 4th step instructions don't explicitly tell you to count the good cans — Mari's sponsor figured out, through years of watching people complete their inventories and then collapse under the weight of them, that something was being lost in translation. So she invented a quiet rule. One good thing per five. And handed it down.

This is how the program actually works at street level. The Big Book is the doctrine; the sponsors are the field engineers who notice what's failing in practice and patch it. A million small interventions like Mari's sponsor's, compounded over ninety years, are what's kept the program working when individual people would have crashed out. The thing you carry forward to the next sponsee isn't always in the book. Sometimes it's something your sponsor told you that they got from theirs.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

  • Father Leo's line of the day — "I danced toward death with God and denial on my lips. Belief in the God of Truth did not stop my dishonesty." Read that twice. The piece is about facts being immune to whether or not we accept them. Alcoholism was killing people long before it had a name. Whatever you've been refusing to face today is probably not less true for being unfaced.
  • Walk in Dry Places on power trips — sometimes "I'm only trying to help" is actually "I'm trying to control." If you've been investing a lot of energy into someone else's recovery, ask yourself whether you'd be okay if they made the choices anyway and didn't take your advice. The honest answer is the data.
  • Bill W. on imagination — alcoholics didn't have an imagination problem. We had a direction problem. The same vivid mind that conjured one more excuse to drink can be pointed at building a sober life. "No man can build a house until he first visions a plan for it." Same engine, different destination.
  • "Easy does it" — re-examined — Twenty-Four Hours notes that alcoholics don't just drink to excess; we do everything to excess. Resentments, worry, exercise, work, opinions. "Easy does it" isn't a slogan about being lazy. It's the literature gently asking you to leave a few cans on the shelf today.
