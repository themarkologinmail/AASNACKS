📖 Today's Featured Reading

As Bill Sees It — "Coping With Anger" — May 14

"Nothing Pays Off Like Restraint of Tongue and Pen"

Few people have been more victimized by resentments than have we alcoholics. A burst of temper could spoil a day, and a well-nursed grudge could make us miserably ineffective. — Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 90

Nor were we ever skillful in separating justified from unjustified anger. As we saw it, our wrath was always justified. Anger, that occasional luxury of more balanced people, could keep us on an emotional jag indefinitely. These "dry benders" often led straight to the bottle.

Nothing pays off like restraint of tongue and pen. We must avoid quick-tempered criticism, furious power-driven argument, sulking, and silent scorn. These are emotional booby traps baited with pride and vengeful emotions. When we are tempted by the bait, we should train ourselves to step back and think. We can neither think nor act to good purpose until the habit of self-restraint has become automatic.

— As Bill Sees It, p. 179, drawn from Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, pp. 90–91

🎯 Deep Dive — The Dry Bender

A "dry bender" is one of those phrases Bill drops casually, in passing, that lands like a small explosion if you actually stop and look at it. He doesn't define it. He doesn't need to. Anyone who's been on one knows.

It's the day you wake up already mad. The week you spend mentally rewriting the email you'll never send. The Sunday afternoon you lose to a conversation in your head with the person you're angry at — running through everything you should have said, everything you'll say next time, every reason you're right. By dinnertime you haven't done anything, you haven't been anywhere, and yet you're exhausted, depleted, and a little meaner than you were yesterday. You haven't had a drink. But something has been drinking you.

Bill's diagnosis is precise: the resentment-engine in an alcoholic mind doesn't run on logic, it runs on fuel. And anger is high-octane fuel. It's not just unpleasant — it's activating. It gives you a sense of purpose, a sense of clarity, a sense of being on the right side of something. That's the trap. The dry bender feels productive. It feels like you're getting somewhere. You're not. You're just driving in circles, fast, with the windows up, breathing your own exhaust.

And here's the move Bill prescribes — and notice how small it is. Not "let it go." Not "forgive them." Not even "do an inventory." Just this: restraint of tongue and pen. Don't say it. Don't write it. Don't send it. Don't post it. Step back and think. That's the whole intervention. The point isn't that you've solved the anger — the point is that you've refused to let it spend you. The grudge gets no oxygen, and eventually it goes out on its own.

There's also a reason Bill names the specific traps: quick-tempered criticism, furious power-driven argument, sulking, silent scorn. Three of those four don't even require speaking. Sulking and silent scorn are dry benders held entirely inside the body. He's telling us: this disease doesn't need a bar to do its work on you. It just needs an unguarded resentment and a few hours of unsupervised mind.

What's the dry bender you're a few hours into right now — and could you put it down before lunch?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What makes this teaching so durable is Bill's refusal to moralize. He doesn't say resentment is wrong, or unspiritual, or unbecoming. He doesn't tell anyone they shouldn't feel angry. He just notices, with the dispassionate attention of someone watching a mechanic diagnose an engine, that this particular fuel makes this particular machine break down. Restraint of tongue and pen isn't a virtue here. It's maintenance.

There's a wider frame worth holding alongside it, too: most of us — alcoholic or not — overestimate how much our anger is doing for us, and underestimate how much it's doing to us. We tell ourselves the grudge is keeping us safe, sharpening our judgment, reminding us not to be fooled again. Most of the time it's doing none of those things. It's just running. And the cost of letting it run, hour after hour, is the day. Sometimes the most spiritual thing you can do is bore a resentment into letting go of you, by refusing to feed it the one thing it needs — your full attention.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Daily Reflections — "It's Okay to Be Me." A clean, no-frills Step 5 reflection, anchored to pp. 72–73 of the Big Book. The line worth carrying: "Humility sounds so much like humiliation, but it really is the ability to look at myself — and honestly accept what I find." If you can't share it in a meeting, share it with a sponsor. Easier methods do not work.
  • Step 11 in the 12&12. The follow-up to yesterday's reading. Bill on the day-throughout use of prayer — pausing at decisions, renewing the request "Thy will, not mine, be done," and repeating a phrase you love until it clears the channel that anger or fear has choked. Prayer as ventilation, not transaction.
  • Today's Gift — the pony in the manure. A father gives his sunny-natured son a Christmas box of hay and animal droppings to teach him a lesson about realism. The boy claps with delight and runs into the backyard: "Yippee! There must be a pony here somewhere!" Reagan used to tell this joke about himself. It's also, accidentally, a pretty good description of what Step 4 turns into when it goes well.
  • Father Leo on sex. One of the warmer, less twitchy pieces on this subject in the daily-meditation canon. The core idea: "compulsive sex is only demonstrated loneliness." Sex outside of gentleness, trust, sensitivity, and communication starts feeding on itself. Worth a sit even — especially — if you wouldn't pick this one off the menu.

P.S. If you're already a few rounds into a dry bender today, try Bill's actual move — not the lofty version. Don't try to forgive. Don't try to feel better about the person. Just don't say it, don't write it, don't send it. Restraint of tongue and pen is a small enough ask that even an angry alcoholic can do it. And nine times out of ten, by sundown, the grudge has lost interest in you first.
