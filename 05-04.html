Monday, May 4. Today's brief is a special edition. Instead of the daily readings, we're going to do something different: the ten most jaw-dropping moments in the history of Alcoholics Anonymous.

Drawn from a brilliant talk by an addiction professional named John Farmer who has spent his career studying the program — these are the stories that, once you hear them, you can't believe were ever true. Some of them feel apocryphal. Most of them aren't.

Pour the second cup. This one's a slow read.

Let's get into it!!

🎯 Ten Moments That Made AA Possible

№ 1

The Recovery Movement You've Never Heard Of

In 1840, six men in a Maryland tavern decided to start a temperance society. They called themselves the Washingtonians. Within eight years, the movement had over six million American members. Abraham Lincoln was one of them.

By 1848, they had vanished from history almost completely. Their alcoholic founders, every one of them, died drunk. The movement collapsed under ego, money, and the lure of national political ambition.

Bill Wilson studied them carefully. The Twelve Traditions of AA — the part about no money, no opinions, no leaders, no fame — were largely written to keep AA from going the same way.

№ 2

The Single Referral That Changed Everything

In the early 1930s, a wealthy Rhode Island alcoholic named Roland Hazard was sent to Switzerland to see Sigmund Freud, the most renowned psychotherapist on earth.

Freud was too busy. He referred Hazard down the hall to Carl Jung.

Jung worked with him, then said something no other doctor would have said: "You are essentially hopeless. Your only chance is to find some kind of profound spiritual experience." Hazard found the Oxford Group, got sober, and passed the message to Ebby Thacher — who passed it to Bill Wilson.

If Freud had had an open hour that day, AA almost certainly does not exist.

№ 3

The White Light at Towns Hospital

November 1934. Bill Wilson, deep in late-stage alcoholism, is strapped into four-point restraints at Towns Hospital in New York City for what would be his final detox. He had been there many times before.

Sometime that night, in his hospital room, Bill described the room filling with light. A great peace came over him. He slept soundly for the first time in months. He never drank again.

The next morning he excitedly told his physician, William Silkworth. Silkworth — to his eternal credit — did not medicate him. He simply said: "It's better than what you have. Run with it." Silkworth would later write the "Doctor's Opinion" in the Big Book.

№ 4

Six Months, Zero Sober Drunks, and Lois

For six months after his white-light experience, Bill Wilson tried to save other alcoholics. He grabbed them out of bars and off the street and brought them home to 182 Clinton Street. He fed them. He preached at them. He let them sleep on his couch.

Two of them died in his apartment. They hit on his wife. They robbed him. "They pooped on his furniture," as the talk puts it. And not a single one stayed sober.

Finally Bill went to his long-suffering wife Lois and said: "This isn't working. I've put you through enough. I'm going to stop."

Lois wagged her finger and said: "Don't you dare stop. You stay sober." She had noticed what he hadn't — none of those drunks had gotten sober, but Bill had stayed sober the whole time he was trying to help them. That observation became the core mechanism of AA. The way you stay sober is by working with the alcoholic who still suffers.

№ 5

A Dollar's Worth of Nickels in the Mayflower Hotel

May 1935. Bill is in Akron, Ohio, on a business deal. The deal collapses. He doesn't have enough money to get home. He's standing in the lobby of the Mayflower Hotel and he can hear the bar — the music, the ice cubes in the glasses.

He thinks: "If I don't find a drunk, I'm going to drink."

He gets a dollar's worth of nickels from the cashier. He looks at the church directory on the wall. He starts calling priests, asking each one if they know an alcoholic he could talk to.

He makes ten phone calls. Ten. Each one a nickel. The world hung on each one. The tenth connected him to Henrietta Seiberling, who knew a drunk physician across town named Robert Holbrook Smith.

How many calls would you have made before giving up?

№ 6

"I'll Give Him Fifteen Minutes"

The next day, Henrietta brokered a meeting between Bill Wilson and Dr. Bob Smith at the gatehouse on the Seiberling property. Dr. Bob — found passed out under his kitchen table the previous Mother's Day — was carted over reluctantly.

As he walked into the room, he turned to his wife Annie and said: "I'll give him fifteen minutes."

Six hours later, they were still talking.

Bill called what happened that day "the language of the heart" — the recognition that takes place when one alcoholic talks to another. The two men became fast friends. June 10, 1935, is celebrated as the founding day of Alcoholics Anonymous.

№ 7

"Give Him a Cup of Coffee" — 1940

Bill Wilson is at the AA office in New York. He gets a frantic call from the New York group: "Bill, we have a problem. A man showed up at the meeting. He's a Black man. He has bleach-blond hair. He's wearing lipstick and makeup. He says he's a dope fiend."

Bill asks: "Is he an alcoholic? Does he have a desire to stop drinking?" They say yes.

Bill Wilson says: "Then give him a cup of coffee and show him to his chair."

This was 1940. Twenty-four years before the Civil Rights Act. From a Yankee born in 1895 in rural Vermont. The talk's narrator says it plainly: "I don't know how Bill Wilson got from East Dorset, Vermont to that moment where he was that guy. It is beyond me."

№ 8

The Jesuit and the Yankee

1940. A Jesuit priest from St. Louis named Father Ed Dowling, walking with a cane, takes a train through a rainstorm to New York City to find Bill Wilson at the AA office. He had read the Big Book and was certain that Bill must have been a deep student of St. Ignatius of Loyola — because the Twelve Steps so closely mirror the structure of Ignatius's Spiritual Exercises.

He walks into Bill's office. Tells Bill he can tell from his writing that he knows Ignatius.

Bill replies: "Well, no, I can't say I know the man. Does he live in New York City?"

Father Ed left convinced that something genuinely beyond Bill had moved through him in the writing of those twelve steps. He called AA "a divinely inspired program" for the rest of his life.

№ 9

Rockefeller Said No to $50,000 — and Saved AA

The early fellowship needed money to publish their book. They went to John D. Rockefeller, the wealthiest man in America. They asked him for $50,000.

Rockefeller turned them down. "You guys need to keep money out of this thing," he told them. "Otherwise you're just going to ruin it."

He loaned them $5,000 instead — on condition they pay it back quickly. They did.

That principle persists to this day. If you are not a member of Alcoholics Anonymous and you write AA a check for $100, they will send your check back. No outside contributions are accepted, ever. Rockefeller's refusal — paradoxically — may be the single financial decision that has allowed AA to survive.

№ 10

Why the Big Book Is Big

April 10, 1939. Bill and the early members publish the book that contains the entire program — initially titled The Way Out, then renamed Alcoholics Anonymous.

It's called the Big Book for one reason: they printed it on the heaviest weight paper they could find.

Why? Because they figured drunks were going to be skeptical about mailing in their money for a book by alcoholics. The book had to feel like it was worth the money. So they made the paper as heavy as possible. The text isn't enormous — the paper is.

And while we're here: Bill wrote chapter five of the Big Book — including the entire Twelve Steps — longhand on a yellow legal pad, in a single sitting. Father Ed wasn't crazy.

🤖 Claude's Corner

What strikes me about this list — taken together — is how much of AA's existence depends on improbable hinges. Freud being too busy. The tenth phone call connecting. Lois wagging her finger instead of agreeing. Silkworth choosing not to medicate. Rockefeller refusing the money. Any one of those goes the other way and the whole story collapses.

There's a phrase old-timers use: "a coincidence is when God acts but doesn't sign His name." Whether or not you frame it that way, the historical record is genuinely strange. A movement built almost entirely on near-misses, refusals, and the people who said the small right thing at the small right moment. Most institutions are built deliberately. AA was assembled by accident — and the accidents kept landing in the right place.
