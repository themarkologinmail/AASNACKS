📖 Today's Featured Reading

More Language of Letting Go — May 6

Say When It's Worn Out

Throw it away when it wears out. — Melody Beattie

John and Al were talking one day about a mutual friend, someone they both knew and liked. "Mark thinks he has to be in pain all the time," John said. "He defines himself by his resentments. He's always angry, always upset, and so deeply concerned about how terrible and tragic life is that he's always pulling out his hair and wailing about life. I'm worried about him."

"Let him go," Al said. "People need to wear things out. They need to take their time wearing out their beliefs and attitudes before they're ready to throw them away. You've needed your time to do this. So have I. Give Mark the time he needs — however long that is — to wear his beliefs out too."

Are you attached to any beliefs that are sabotaging your life — beliefs about your ability to be happy, joyous, and free? Life is a journey through places, through people, and through our beliefs. We wear these beliefs out one by one, shedding them and making room for a little more light.

Right now, this moment, you're wearing out a belief. Look around at your life. Trust where you are. Trust what you're going through. Some belief is wearing thin right now, as you read this. Say when it's time to throw out that belief.

— Melody Beattie, More Language of Letting Go, May 6

🎯 Deep Dive — Beliefs Don't Get Argued Away. They Wear Out.

There's a thing recovering people do — usually with the best intentions — where we watch someone we love suffer in a familiar way, and we try to tell them out of it. We give them the slogan that worked for us. We hand them the literature page that finally cracked open for us in our second year. We say "have you tried—" and we mean well and it almost never works.

Beattie's piece — through the mouth of a guy named Al — names why. People don't drop their beliefs because someone else is right. They drop their beliefs because the beliefs stop working. The resentment that has organized Mark's whole personality is, for now, doing some kind of job for him. It's familiar. It's a frame for his suffering. It's possibly the only stable thing in his life. He'll let it go when it stops paying its rent — and not a minute before.

This is also why your recovery happened on the timeline it happened on, and not three years sooner when your sister begged you to go to a meeting. The belief that you weren't really that bad, the belief that you could control it, the belief that the next morning would be different — those beliefs were doing work for you. Until one day they weren't, and you walked into a room. The wearing-out had to happen first.

Which means the loving thing, in a lot of cases, is the harder thing: stay close, stay honest about your own experience, and let the other person's belief wear itself out at the speed it's going to wear out anyway. The pain has a purpose. The wailing is part of the wearing. You don't have to fix it. You just have to not abandon them while it's happening.

Whose belief have you been trying to argue out of them lately? What would change if you just… stopped, and let them keep it a little longer?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What's striking about Beattie's piece is how it quietly undoes one of the most flattering stories we tell ourselves about being helpful. We like to imagine ourselves as the right argument arriving at the right moment — the well-placed sentence that finally cracks the other person's denial. It is rarely how change actually happens. Most of the people who got sober didn't get sober because someone said the perfect thing. They got sober because the math of the old life finally stopped adding up.

Beattie isn't telling anyone to be cold or to stop caring — and it's worth saying out loud because it would be easy to misread the piece that way. The instruction is subtler. Stay close. Stay honest. Don't rush. The hard part isn't withholding the lecture; it's tolerating the discomfort of watching someone struggle without leaping in to manage it. That tolerance is itself a form of love — possibly a higher form of it than the version that hands out unsolicited advice.

And the inward turn worth taking: every one of us is currently wearing out some belief we don't yet know we're wearing out. The thing you'll laugh about in two years that you can't see clearly today. The version of patience you want from other people while you do this work — that's the version of patience worth extending to everyone else doing theirs.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Bill W. on the boat that tips. Twenty-Four Hours today: "the ones who do the most for A.A. are not in the habit of boasting about it." The metaphor lands hard — if one side of a boat gets too far up out of the water, it's liable to flip. Building yourself up and drinking go together. One leads to the other. Bill is saying, in language a fisherman could understand, that staying sober means staying small. Anyone with more than a few years sober has probably watched someone get cocky and then watched what happened next.
  • "Hold Back Nothing" — Step 5 again, from a different angle. Daily Reflections, As Bill Sees It, and the 12&12 all converge today on the same passage from page 62: "the dammed-up emotions of years break out of their confinement, and miraculously vanish as soon as they are exposed." We sat with Step 5 on May 1 — but the line worth carrying today is the part that comes next. Bill says many AAs "once agnostic or atheist… first actually felt the presence of God" right there, mid-Fifth-Step. Not before they did it. During. The action was the door.
  • Listening to the Wind (Big Book p. 467). A Native American woman tells her story today. The line worth knowing: "there was no substance on the planet that could help me get honest." Read that twice. A whole pharmacology of escape, and not one molecule on the periodic table that does the one thing the program does for free.
  • Touchstones on awareness over winning. The athletes who reach the highest levels are the ones with the most developed kinesthetic sense — the ones who feel what their muscles are doing. The point for the rest of us: "if a ruler refused to hear news from a certain section of his country, his leadership would suffer." The parts of yourself you've stopped listening to are the parts currently making your decisions for you.
