📖 Today's Featured Reading

Alcoholics Anonymous — "A Drunk, Like You" — May 13

"Say Something Else That You Like"

The more he listened at meetings, the more he came to know about his own drinking history. — Alcoholics Anonymous (Fourth Edition), p. 406

The last big hurdle was closing the meeting with the Lord's Prayer. As a Jew, I was uncomfortable with it and decided to talk to my sponsor about it.

So I said, "The Lord's Prayer bothers me."

"Oh," he said, "what's the problem?"

"Well, I'm Jewish and it's not a Jewish prayer."

"Well, then," he said, "say it in Jewish."

I said, "It would still be the Lord's Prayer."

"Right," he said. "Then say something else that you like. Your Higher Power, whatever you call it, is helping you, and you need to say thank you."

— Alcoholics Anonymous (the "Big Book"), Fourth Edition, p. 406, from the story "A Drunk, Like You" in the section "They Stopped in Time"

🎯 Deep Dive — The Whole "Religion in AA" Problem, Solved in Seven Words

Notice what the sponsor doesn't do. He doesn't defend the prayer. He doesn't explain its history. He doesn't argue that it's "really" universal, or remind his sponsee that the rooms are non-denominational, or get defensive about Christianity in AA. He doesn't even tell him he has to say it. He just makes one small move: "Say something else that you like."

That move contains the entire AA position on religion in a single sentence. The prayer isn't the point. The thank you is the point. The form is interchangeable; the practice is not. As the sponsee says a few lines later in his story: religion is the ritual, and we all differ there, and spirituality is the way we feel about what we do.

This is what makes AA portable across faiths in a way almost nothing else manages. The program never asks you to import a theology. It asks you to do something — show up, make amends, get honest, say thank you — and then it gets out of the way about exactly how you do it. A Jewish member can stand in a circle full of Christians, hold hands, and say a private prayer of gratitude in Hebrew, in silence, or in his own words. The structure works. The God of his understanding is the one who shows up.

And here's the move worth carrying out of this story: the next time something in the program scrapes against your beliefs, your background, your taste — don't try to fix the form. Find the practice underneath it. The form is just the container. What matters is whether you're actually doing the thing the form was pointing at.

Whatever your Higher Power is — when's the last time you actually said thank you?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What's so quietly remarkable about this exchange is the sponsor's trust — trust that his sponsee can find his own way to gratitude without being told what to say. Most spiritual instruction errs in the opposite direction: here is the right prayer; here is the wrong prayer; here is the right name for God. The sponsor, by contrast, hands the practice back to the person doing it. The God you've got is enough. The thank-you you've got is enough. You don't need a translation.

There's an old idea worth holding alongside this: the finger pointing at the moon is not the moon. A prayer is a finger. So is a meditation, a meeting format, a step, a slogan. None of them are the thing itself. They're all pointing at the same moon — that small, private moment of contact between a person and whatever it is that's bigger than them. The sponsor's gift to his sponsee was permission to put down the finger he'd been arguing with and just look at the moon. Find the practice that lets you actually look up.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Step 11 lands in the 12&12 — and Bill's quiet correction to how most people pray: stop asking for specific outcomes "our way," and start adding the five-word qualifier "if it be Thy will" to every request. Then ask, mostly, for two things: knowledge of His will, and the power to carry it out. That's it. That's the prayer.
  • Walk in Dry Places — "Who's to blame?" The honest line: "however accurate it may be, such blame-placing does nothing constructive. It really serves only to reinforce our bitterness and resentment." You can be 100% right about who wronged you and still be the one paying interest on it.
  • Daily Reflections — "The Easier, Softer Way." A note on Step 5, and the alcoholic who kept coming back to meetings trembling from the horrors of reliving his past because he wouldn't take the Fifth. Sometimes the "easier" way is just rebranded suffering.
  • Father Leo on arguments. "Why did I argue so much? Usually it is because I feel threatened, angry, discounted — or I am wrong and I do not want to admit it." One of those lines that makes the next argument you almost have feel optional.
