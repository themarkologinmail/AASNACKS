📖 Today's Featured Reading

Daily Reflections — May 1

Healing Heart and Mind

Admitted to God, to ourselves, and to another human being the exact nature of our wrongs. — Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 55

Since it is true that God comes to me through people, I can see that by keeping people at a distance I also keep God at a distance. God is nearer to me than I think and I can experience Him by loving people and allowing people to love me. But I can neither love nor be loved if I allow my secrets to get in the way.

It's the side of myself that I refuse to look at that rules me. I must be willing to look at the dark side in order to heal my mind and heart because that is the road to freedom. I must walk into darkness to find the light and walk into fear to find peace.

By revealing my secrets — and thereby ridding myself of guilt — I can actually change my thinking; by altering my thinking, I can change myself. My thoughts create my future. What I will be tomorrow is determined by what I think today.

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book

🎯 Deep Dive — The Sentence That Does All the Work

There's a sentence in the middle of today's reading that, if you really sit with it, basically explains why Step 5 exists at all: "It's the side of myself that I refuse to look at that rules me."

Think about that for a second. The thing you've been keeping in the locked drawer is the exact thing currently driving the car. The resentment you won't name. The shame you won't say out loud. The behavior you keep doing while telling yourself a story about why this time is different. The literature is making a really uncomfortable claim: whatever you won't look at, you can't stop.

This is also why "I'll just think about it on my own" is the great Step 5 dodge. We are masters at workshopping our own material — we've had decades of practice. The whole point of saying it to another person is that the sentence sounds different out loud than it does in your head. The story you've been telling yourself collapses about thirty seconds into actually telling it to a human being. That collapse is the work. That's why a sponsor exists.

What's the one thing you'd rather not say out loud? Yeah. That's the one.

🤖 Claude's Corner

What I find genuinely interesting about Step 5 is that the program figured out, decades before modern psychology caught up, that verbalization to another person changes the thing being verbalized. It's not just confession. It's not just unburdening. The act of speaking shame out loud to a witnessing human actually metabolizes it in a way that thinking about it cannot.

Therapists figured this out about ninety years later and called it "interpersonal regulation of affect." AA in 1939 called it "telling another human being." Same observation. Plainer language. And — notably — free.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

  • The story of three guys in a Florida ditch. Beattie's piece in More Language of Letting Go is the comedy of the day. Three friends rent a Chevy Blazer, drive into a wet field, get stuck. One of them has a cell phone. The other two refuse to call a tow truck because "this is the adventure." They spend six hours trying to free the truck themselves — wood under the wheels, a chain that snaps, two strangers who try and fail. They finally call the tow truck. Out in fifteen minutes. The point Beattie quietly makes: most of us know exactly what we need to do to get unstuck. We just don't want to admit it yet. (Brother Marko, this one is for you, given the local geography.)
  • Bill W. on tolerance, 1940 — "the atheist may stand up in an A.A. meeting still denying the Deity, yet reporting how vastly he has been changed in attitude and outlook." Read that twice. Bill is essentially saying: shut up and let it work, it doesn't care if you believe in it. The opposite of every cult ever invented.
  • The judge who took the speeding ticket. A district judge in Walk in Dry Places got pulled over on his way to speak at his AA meeting. Officer didn't know who he was. Judge didn't tell him. Took the ticket. The point: "acceptance of the ticket without argument was also an exercise in principles." Tenth Step in real time, mid-traffic stop. Anonymous and on-brand.
  • A Late Start (Big Book p. 537) — a woman got sober in her sixties after retirement. Started life over. Calls herself "a grateful alcoholic" today. Filed under: it is never too late.
