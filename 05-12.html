🌴 How AA Came to Miami

April 1940. A man named Roger G., living in Miami, writes to GSO in New York — then called the Alcoholic Foundation — asking for help with his drinking. He gets a letter back, some literature, and a connection.

November 1940. Another Miami man, Joe T., writes the same office with the same problem. GSO does the simple thing: it puts Roger and Joe in touch with each other. Two strangers in a city of 172,000 people, both desperate, both writing to New York for help, both told here's the other one.

They start answering inquiries from the Miami area together. They're helped by a man named Irwin M., a salesman whose territory covers the Southeast U.S. — he's a sober AA member, and when his route brings him through, he advises them in person and by letter when it doesn't.

December 1940. Carl C. arrives from Houston, settles in Fort Lauderdale, and wants in. GSO connects him to Roger and Joe. Local newspapers run a series of articles about this strange new movement called Alcoholics Anonymous. The three men do twelfth-step work for anyone who calls.

December 23, 1940. The first official AA meeting in Miami is held. The group formally registers with GSO in April 1941, and that date is celebrated as Miami's anniversary. By the end of 1941, the directory lists 40 members in Miami.

It moved fast from there. By February 29, 1944 — a leap day — Miami AA was strong enough to send a delegation north. Dick R., Fred C., and a woman whose name nobody bothered to write down drove up to Fort Lauderdale and held Broward County's first-ever meeting. The Miamians spoke. The Fort Lauderdale alcoholics listened. They closed with the Lord's Prayer. Broward got planted by Miami.

Today, Miami-Dade is District 10 of South Florida Area 15, with its own intergroup running a 24/7 helpline in English and Spanish. And here's where the story gets really good — because what Miami has done with the program in the last sixty years is something most of AA's founding cities never managed.

Miami runs the program in at least four languages. The English fellowship is one wing of the house. The Spanish fellowship is another wing — arguably the larger one, with dozens of grupos hispanohablantes meeting daily across Hialeah, Little Havana, Kendall, Doral, and Homestead. The names alone are a small poem: Fe y Esperanza (Faith and Hope). Cuento Contigo (I Count On You). Amistad (Friendship). Liberación. Resurrección. Quinta Tradición. Háblame de Ti (Tell Me About You). And the one that lands hardest of all — El Lenguaje del Corazón, "the language of the heart," the exact phrase Bill W. used in 1935 to describe what passed between him and Dr. Bob in Akron. It traveled.

The Spanish helpline — 305-642-2805 — is answered around the clock. La Viña, the Spanish-language version of AA Grapevine, circulates as widely here as the English edition. There's a Russian-speaking AA group serving South Florida, sober Russian-speakers passing the book back and forth. Haitian Creole meetings exist. French meetings too. Hybrid Zoom rooms hold members in Miami, Havana, Buenos Aires, and Madrid in the same hour.

None of this was planned. It grew because the program works in any language a suffering alcoholic happens to think in. The same simple thing Roger and Joe did in 1940 — write to a stranger, ask for help, meet another sufferer — got repeated in Spanish a thousand times, in Russian a few hundred, in Creole more quietly. Tradition 3 turned out to be a passport: the only requirement for membership is a desire to stop drinking. Not the language. Not the papers. Not the country of origin. The chain runs unbroken across all of it: Roger and Joe wrote to New York. Carl wrote to Roger. The Miamians drove to Fort Lauderdale. Someone, somewhere, called the Spanish line at 2 a.m. Someone else answered. You'll call someone to theirs.

Sources: South Florida Area 15 Archives Committee, "Our Beginnings: Bits of AA History," compiled by Maria Hoffman, District 1 Archives Chair; Miami-Dade Intergroup (aamiamidade.org); District 10 Miami (district10miami.org).

📖 Today's Reading

Daily Reflections — May 12

The Past Is Over

A.A. experience has taught us we cannot live alone with our pressing problems and the character defects which cause or aggravate them. If Step Four has revealed in stark relief those experiences we'd rather not remember… then the need to quit living by ourselves with those tormenting ghosts of yesterday gets more urgent than ever. We have to talk to somebody about them. — Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 55

Whatever is done is over. It cannot be changed. But my attitude about it can be changed through talking with those who have gone before and with sponsors. I can wish the past never was, but if I change my actions in regard to what I have done, my attitude will change. I won't have to wish the past away. I can change my feelings and attitudes, but only through my actions and the help of my fellow alcoholics.

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book, written by AA members

🎯 Deep Dive — Roger and Joe

Read the reading and the history together and you find the same teaching twice — first as instruction, then as proof. Bill in the 12&12 writes, "we cannot live alone with our pressing problems." Eight years earlier, in Miami, Roger G. and Joe T. independently arrived at the same conclusion and acted on it. They didn't have a sponsor. They didn't have a meeting. They had a return address in New York and the willingness to use it.

What's striking is how small the act was. Roger didn't get sober by writing a letter. He got sober — or at least walked toward sobriety — because writing the letter was the moment he stopped pretending he could do it alone. The letter was tiny. The shift behind it was the whole program. And once that shift happened, GSO did the one thing the alcoholic mind never does: it gave him a phone number for another sufferer in the same city. Here's the other one.

The reading puts it as a daily practice. The "tormenting ghosts of yesterday" — the 4th-step material, the stuff you'd rather not remember — don't get exorcised by hiding them better. They get smaller when they get spoken. Out loud. To another human. Preferably one who has been through their own version. The mechanism is the same in 2026 Miami as it was in 1940: the thing you cannot do alone becomes possible the moment two of you are doing it together.

If Roger and Joe needed each other in a city of 172,000, what makes you think you don't?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What's quietly beautiful about the Miami story is how unspectacular it is. No white-light experience. No Mayflower Hotel moment. No Father Ed Dowling walking through the rain. Just two men writing letters, a salesman with a sales route, and a meeting in someone's house three days before Christmas. Most of AA's growth, then and now, has looked like this. Not famous moments. Small, repeated, faithful acts of connection.

A frame worth holding: the spiritual life rarely arrives as an event. It arrives as a pattern. Roger writing the letter. Joe writing the letter. Irwin driving the route. Carl moving to Florida. The leap-day delegation to Broward. A Spanish line answered in 1965 or 1975 or last night. Each motion is ordinary. The pattern is the miracle. And the pattern only happens because, somewhere along the line, a person decided to stop trying to be the exception — the one who could do it alone — and became, instead, a link. The links are the chain. The chain is the program. Today, you're one of the links.
