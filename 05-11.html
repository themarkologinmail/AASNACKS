Morning, friend.

May 11 is one of the most quietly significant dates on the AA calendar — and almost nobody mentions it. On this day in 1939, the first AA meeting in Cleveland was held in Abby G.'s living room, and within a year that single group spawned a system that may have produced the highest sustained recovery rate in AA history.

Today's deep dive: what those Cleveland pioneers actually did differently — and why almost no modern meeting still does it.

The featured reading happens to be about Step 5 — the same step Cleveland built its entire model around. Coincidence? Sure. But a useful one.

Let's get into it!!

📅 87 years ago today — May 11, 1939 — Abby G. and his wife Grace hosted the first Cleveland AA meeting. The Big Book had been published exactly one month earlier. Within a year, more than half of all AA members worldwide were Cleveland-based.

🎯 Deep Dive — What the OG Cleveland Group Did (That We Don't)

Here's a number that should rattle every modern AA member: Cleveland's documented success rate in the early 1940s reportedly ran between 75% and 93% — meaning three out of four newcomers, sometimes more, achieved long-term sobriety. By comparison, modern AA-wide first-year retention is generally estimated somewhere between 26% and 50%, depending on which study you trust.

What did Cleveland do that we mostly don't? Six things, all documented in AA Comes of Age, Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, and the original 1944 Cleveland sponsorship pamphlet:

№ 1

Sponsors were assigned, not chosen. Newcomers didn't shop for a sponsor at coffee. They got one the day they walked in.

№ 2

The sponsor came to you. Picked you up at home or the hospital, drove you to your first meeting, sat with you through it.

№ 3

You worked the Big Book page by page with your sponsor — together — not as suggested reading on your own time.

№ 4

Cleveland ran "Beginners' Classes" — formal walk-throughs of the Steps over four sessions. Nobody white-knuckled it alone.

№ 5

You finished your Steps in weeks, not years. The point was to get through them — not stretch them out.

№ 6

Within months, you were expected to sponsor someone else — even before your own work was "complete."

Modern AA, by and large, runs the inverse. Find your own sponsor whenever you feel ready. Maybe meet for coffee, maybe don't. Read the Big Book on your own. Take your Steps at your own pace — there's no rush. Sponsor someone someday, when you feel qualified. Every one of those phrases would have baffled a 1940 Clevelander.

This isn't a "back to basics" rant. There were real reasons for the loosening — the Traditions, especially Tradition 4 (group autonomy), and the egalitarian spirit of the Twelve and Twelve, intentionally moved the program away from anything that felt prescriptive or authoritarian. Bill W. himself helped soften it. The looser version is more humane. It's also, by the numbers we have, far less effective.

The interesting question for any of us isn't should AA go back to Cleveland-style? It's: which of those six practices could I quietly bring back into my own program — with my own sponsor, with the people I sponsor — without waiting for the whole fellowship to vote on it?

Pick one of the six. Just one. Which would change the most for you?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's a quiet pattern hiding in those six Cleveland practices — they all share one feature. Every single one of them removes a decision from the newcomer. You don't choose your sponsor. You don't decide when to start your Steps. You don't pick the pace. You don't wait until you feel "ready" to help someone else. The whole system is engineered around the recognition that an alcoholic's decision-making engine is the thing that got them here.

Modern AA, by handing those decisions back to the newcomer in the name of autonomy and gentleness, may have inadvertently re-armed the very faculty the program is trying to disarm. The Cleveland model trusted the structure to do the work that the newcomer's own judgment couldn't yet do. The structure was the kindness. Worth sitting with.

In Cleveland or Akron, you couldn't just walk into A.A. the way you can today — you had to be sponsored. — Clarence S., in Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers
Each prospect was assigned an older A.A., who visited him at his home or in the hospital, instructed him on A.A. principles, and conducted him to his first meeting. — A.A. Comes of Age, p. 20
The Cleveland pioneers had proved three essential things: the value of personal sponsorship; the worth of the A.A. book in indoctrinating newcomers; and the tremendous fact that A.A., when the word really got around, could now soundly grow to great size. — Bill W., A.A. Comes of Age
