📖 Today's Featured Reading

Daily Reflections — May 10

FREE AT LAST

Another great dividend we may expect from confiding our defects to another human being is humility — a word often misunderstood … it amounts to a clear recognition of what and who we really are, followed by a sincere attempt to become what we could be. — Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 58

I knew deep inside that if I were ever to be joyous, happy and free, I had to share my past life with some other individual. The joy and relief I experienced after doing so were beyond description. Almost immediately after taking the Fifth Step, I felt free from the bondage of self and the bondage of alcohol. That freedom remains after 36 years, a day at a time. I found that God could do for me what I couldn't do for myself.

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book, written by AA members

🎯 Deep Dive — Humility Isn't What You Think It Is

Most people walk into AA with a definition of humility they got from somewhere else — usually a Sunday school class or a boss who wanted them quiet. Humility means thinking less of yourself. Humility means keeping your head down. Humility means agreeing you're a worm. Then they get to Step 5, do the inventory, share it with another human being, and brace for the part where they're supposed to feel small. And what arrives instead is the line in today's reading.

"A clear recognition of what and who we really are, followed by a sincere attempt to become what we could be." Read it twice. There's no self-flagellation in there. No worm. No worthlessness. Humility, in the program, is two clean parts: see yourself accurately, then try, sincerely, to become the version of you that you sense you could be. That's it. That's the entire definition.

Notice what the program is doing here. It's refusing the fake humility of I'm nothing, because that's just inverted ego — still all about you, just dressed in sackcloth. And it's refusing the inflated self-image too — the version where you skip the inventory because the truth might be uncomfortable. Both of those are dishonest postures. Real humility is the boring middle: see what's actually there, defects and gifts, then quietly work in the direction of better.

This is why Step 5 produces the freedom the writer describes. You can't see what and who you really are in your own head — your head is the room where the distortions live. You have to say it out loud, to another person, with the lights on. The moment you do, the spell breaks. The shameful thing turns out to be human. The grand thing turns out to be ordinary. The bondage of self loosens because self stopped getting to write its own story unchecked. That's the dividend. That's the freedom. Thirty-six years of it, and counting.

If humility just meant "see yourself accurately, then try to become better" — would you accept it? Or are you still attached to a more dramatic version?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What's quietly remarkable about Bill W.'s definition of humility is how operational it is. Most spiritual traditions describe humility in terms of feeling — feeling small, feeling lowly, feeling empty. The 12&12 instead describes it in terms of action: see clearly, then move toward who you could be. It's a definition you can actually do something with on a Tuesday afternoon.

There's also something worth pausing on in the phrase "what and who we really are." Two different words, doing different work. What we are — the diagnosis, the patterns, the honest assessment of the machinery. Who we are — the person underneath, the one capable of becoming. The program isn't asking you to confuse them. It's asking you to know both, hold them at the same time, and let one quietly lead toward the other. Accurate seeing plus directional movement. That's the whole thing.

😂 The Funniest AA Jokes (A Special Section)

The rooms run on coffee, surrender, and self-deprecating humor — pretty much in that order. Here are the ones that keep getting passed around because they're actually true, which is what makes them funny.

№ 1 — The Whole Program in One Sentence

"Religion is for people who are afraid of going to hell. Spirituality is for people who've already been there."

— heard at every speaker meeting at least once
№ 2 — The Newcomer's Question

A newcomer asks, "How long do I have to come to these meetings?" The old-timer says, "Just until you start liking them — then you have to keep coming for the rest of your life."

№ 3 — On the Steps

"The first three Steps are: I can't, He can, let Him. The next nine are: well, fine, I'll do my part."

№ 4 — On Self-Knowledge

"I came to AA to save my ass — and discovered it was attached to my soul."

— attributed to many; perfected by all of them
№ 5 — On Resentments

"Holding a resentment is like drinking poison and waiting for the other person to die."

— not technically a joke until you remember you've done it
№ 6 — The Geography Lesson

"My sponsor told me my problem isn't that I drink too much. My problem is that I think too much, and drinking is the only thing that shuts it up. Fix the thinking, and the drink takes care of itself. I told her that was the dumbest thing I'd ever heard. Six months later, I asked her to repeat it because I couldn't remember exactly how she'd put it."

№ 7 — On Higher Power

"There are two things you need to know about God: there is one, and you're not it."

№ 8 — On Patience

"I prayed for patience and God gave me a sponsor who calls back exactly when she feels like it."

№ 9 — On Old-Timers

A guy with thirty days walks up to a guy with thirty years and says, "How'd you do it?" The old-timer says, "Don't drink, go to meetings, ask for help. That's the program." The newcomer says, "That's it? That's all?" The old-timer says, "Yeah. That's why nobody does it."

№ 10 — The One That Gets You Every Time

"My best thinking got me here."

— funny the first time, devastating the second, useful forever
