A daily brief for Spiritual Miners

Morning, friend.

May 3. Today's brief sits with a piece of writing that's nearly fifty years old, fierce, and still controversial inside the rooms.

In 1976, a former General Service Office staffer named Tom Powers wrote that "weak AA is driving out strong AA" — and that most contemporary members aren't really practicing the program at all.

It's a strong piece, written from love of the program. Some of it will land. Some of it will sit unfamiliarly. The most useful thing we can do this morning is read it openly and let it work on us as it will.

Let's get into it!!

📖 Today's Featured Reading

24 Magazine — July 1976

Gresham's Law in Alcoholics Anonymous

Bad currency drives out good. Weak AA is tending to drive out strong AA. — Tom Powers, eleven-year AA member, former GSO staffer

The piece offers, in his own framing: there are three ways to work the AA program. Strong AA — all twelve steps, practiced rigorously and continuously. Medium AA — a strong start that softens after a year or two into a meeting a week and occasional service. Weak AA (he calls it "copped-out and watered-down" or COWD) — meeting attendance, abstinence, and not much else.

He observes that AA worked spectacularly in its first decade because the original members had no choice but to practice all the steps. There was no medium option, no weak option. The Twelve Steps as Bill W. first drafted them were instructions, not suggestions. The "suggested as a program of recovery" framing in chapter five was a late compromise — one he believes Bill himself was uneasy about.

What changed, he argues, was scale. Once AA exploded after the 1939 Cleveland Plain Dealer articles and the 1941 Saturday Evening Post piece, the fellowship became big enough that newcomers could stay sober simply by joining the social organization — by what Powers calls mimesis, the imitation of the people around them. They didn't need a spiritual conversion. They needed an Elks club where the members happened not to drink.

What most concerns him is one word change in the Twelfth Step. The original 1939 wording said "having had a spiritual experience as the result of these steps." By the early 1940s it had been softened to "having had a spiritual awakening." An appendix was added explaining that the change wasn't required to be dramatic. He reads this as the moment AA began accommodating less rigorous practice and lowering its spiritual aspirations.

What he sees ahead: as the years pass and weak AA crowds out strong AA, the recovery rate will fall, old-timers will lose their joy, and the program will slowly drift toward what every other large spiritual institution eventually becomes — respectable, organizationally healthy, and spiritually hollow. "Even the best of human institutions tend to deteriorate in time."

What he hopes for has two parts. The first is what almost any sponsor would say to any drifting member: practice all the steps. Keep practicing them. Don't rest on what you did in your first six months. The second is larger — a return, fellowship-wide, to what he calls original AA, where the steps are treated as essentials. In his own words: "The men who wrote the Big Book were, as it turns out, right after all. There is no easier, softer way."

— Tom Powers, "Gresham's Law in Alcoholics Anonymous," 24 Magazine, July 1976

🎯 Deep Dive — What's Worth Carrying From This

There's a great deal here worth carrying.

Drift is real. Almost every long-sober member, if they're being candid, will tell you the work changed at some point. Less inventory. Less prayer. Less Twelfth-Step service. The intensity that got us through the first year quietly relaxed into the comfortable rhythm that keeps us through the tenth. Powers isn't inventing a phenomenon. He's naming one.

"Suggestions only" can become a dodge. The framing that allows reluctant newcomers to approach the steps without being scared off is the same framing that lets long-timers quietly drop the parts they find uncomfortable and call it "working it their own way." When everything is optional, nothing is essential — and recovery requires some essentials. If your version of the program has quietly omitted Steps 4 and 5 for the past seven years, that's worth noticing.

Mimesis is observable. People do, in fact, stay sober for stretches because their social circle now consists of people who don't drink. That's not nothing — it's actually a big deal, and it saves lives. But it's also structurally fragile. The newcomer whose sobriety rests primarily on social conformity rather than internal transformation is the one most likely to relapse when life shocks arrive — divorce, death, loss of community, geographic move. The steps exist to build something more durable than the room you're sitting in.

Old-timers do struggle. The phenomenon Powers names — long-sober members searching for the old fire and finding boredom, brittleness, or worse — is documented in decades of AA writing. It has a name in the rooms: "dry drunk." Bill himself wrote about it, repeatedly. If you've been in the program a long time and the joy has quietly leaked out, Powers is at least pointing at something real about why.

At its center, the piece offers one good question: are you actually still doing the work, or are you coasting on what you did in your first six months? That question deserves to be sat with by every member, regardless of how they hold the rest of the essay.

Sit with the question. The renewal it points to is the work.

🤖 Claude's Corner — A Wider Frame

Sitting with this piece, what stands out is how much of it lands cleanly. The drift he names is observable. The teaching has softened. "Don't drink and go to meetings" — said at meetings constantly — is not what the Big Book actually prescribes. Watered-down practice often fails the real alcoholic the program was written for. The strong/medium/weak typology, taken as a description of practice patterns rather than a verdict on people, is a useful diagnostic tool. None of that is in dispute.

What's worth holding alongside the piece is a wider meditation on what makes the diagnosis sharp without making it brittle.

Consider what a spiritual experience actually is. The Big Book describes it as moving from one way of being into another. Not a doctrine. Not a creed. A change in who you are. From darkness of being to lightness of being. From self-obsession to availability for love. From the locked room of the alcoholic mind to the open air of a life lived with other people. The point in the essay is that this movement requires the full program to produce reliably — and the literature itself agrees. Meetings without steps do not, on their own, generate that movement. Fellowship is the soil. The steps are the seed. Both are required.

William James understood this almost a century ago, and Bill leaned heavily on his work. James described two valid forms of spiritual change: the sudden upheaval and the slow educational variety. Both are real. Both transformative. Neither is a substitute for actually doing the work — they're two shapes the work can take in a person's life.

The historical record on recovery rates also supports the diagnosis more than it complicates it. The 1955 Big Book Foreword reported that, of those who came to A.A. and really tried, 50% got sober at once and remained that way; 25% sobered up after some relapses, and among the remainder, those who stayed on showed improvement. That figure describes an era when the program was practiced at full strength. The most rigorous current evidence — a 2020 Cochrane systematic review by John Kelly of Harvard, Keith Humphreys of Stanford, and Marica Ferri (27 studies, 10,565 participants) — found that AA and Twelve-Step Facilitation produce about 42% continuous abstinence at one year, compared with 35% for cognitive-behavioral therapy, with the advantage persisting through three years. Project MATCH, the largest psychotherapy trial in history, found similar advantages. What this evidence shows is that AA still works at meaningful rates, especially compared with clinical alternatives. What it doesn't show is whether the practice within AA has held its original depth. That's the question being asked, and the modern data doesn't really answer it.

And finally, the one piece worth holding lightly. Any sharp diagnostic framework — including a useful one — comes with a question of how it gets used. The strong/medium/weak typology serves best as a self-diagnostic: am I drifting from what works? That use is loving accountability and the heart of what sponsors do. The same framework, applied member-to-member across a meeting room — that person isn't doing real AA — is a different motion entirely. Not because the diagnostic is wrong, but because extremism breeds intolerance of other people's beliefs and practices, and any rigorous framework requires humility in how it's wielded toward others. The piece is asking each of us to apply the diagnostic to ourselves. That's the right use. The misuse — applying it across the room — is the failure mode worth being awake to, in any of us.

The honest synthesis: the essay names something real. The drift is real, the dilution is real, the watered-down version isn't enough for a real alcoholic, and the call to renew the practice is well-founded. The renewal is each member's own work. Take the diagnosis. Apply it inward. Let the program do its work on you. The wisest version of the call to deeper practice is the version that turns it on yourself first.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

  • The Tradition Three story — in the early days, an alcoholic showed up to AA who was also a "sex deviate" by the standards of 1937. The group panicked. Bill said: "What would the Master do?" They let him in. He recovered. From that moment, the only requirement became the desire to stop drinking. The whole program's spirit is in that one decision.
  • Bill W. on tolerance, 1940 (quoted yesterday but worth reprising today) — "the atheist may stand up in an A.A. meeting still denying the Deity, yet reporting how vastly he has been changed in attitude and outlook." Bill is essentially saying: shut up and let it work, it doesn't care if you do it the way you think you're supposed to.
  • The "dry drunk" phenomenon — sober but miserable, abstinent but not free. Some read it as proof the program is being practiced too lightly. Others read it as a passage many members move through on the long road, with the way out looking different for different people. Both readings can be true at once for different members on different mornings.
  • And the line for today, from chapter five — "We claim spiritual progress rather than spiritual perfection." Read it twice if you read today's featured piece this morning. The fellowship lives in the gap between progress and perfection — pulled toward both, beholden to neither.
