📖 Today's Featured Reading

Daily Reflections — May 9

Walking Through Fear

If we still cling to something we will not let go, we ask God to help us be willing. — Alcoholics Anonymous, p. 76

When I had taken my Fifth Step, I became aware that all my defects of character stemmed from my need to feel secure and loved. To use my will alone to work on them would have been trying obsessively to solve the problem.

In the Sixth Step I intensified the action I had taken in the first three Steps — meditating on the Step by saying it over and over, going to meetings, following my sponsor's suggestions, reading and searching within myself.

During the first three years of sobriety I had a fear of entering an elevator alone. One day I decided I must walk through this fear. I asked for God's help, entered the elevator, and there in the corner was a lady crying. She said that since her husband had died she was deathly afraid of elevators. I forgot my fear and comforted her.

This spiritual experience helped me to see how willingness was the key to working the rest of the Twelve Steps to recovery.

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book, written by AA members

🎯 Deep Dive — The Cure for Fear Isn't Less Fear

There's a quiet detail in this story that's easy to miss. The woman had been sober three years when she walked into that elevator. That's not a newcomer story. That's a person well into the work — meetings, sponsor, steps — who still couldn't get on an elevator by herself. Three years of sobriety did not, by itself, fix the fear.

Most of us imagine that recovery works by gradually shrinking our fears down until they disappear. Enough meetings, enough prayer, enough steps, and one day you'll walk into the thing that scared you and feel nothing. But the literature, again and again, says something different. The fear didn't shrink. It just stopped being the loudest thing in the room. The cure for fear isn't less fear. It's a bigger reason to walk forward anyway.

And the bigger reason, in this story, was a stranger who needed her. The moment she stepped into the elevator and saw a woman crying, the calculation flipped. Her fear didn't matter as much as the other woman's grief did. "I forgot my fear and comforted her." That word "forgot" is doing a lot of work. The fear didn't get conquered. It got demoted. It went from being the center of her experience to being a footnote — because something more important had walked into the same small room with her.

This is why service is treated as medicine in the program, not as virtue. The Big Book isn't asking you to help others because you're a generous person. It's pointing out that self-obsession is the engine of fear, and the most reliable way to interrupt self-obsession is to be useful to someone else for ten minutes. You don't think your way out of fear. You walk into the elevator with willingness, and then you see who else is in there.

What's the elevator you've been refusing to get on? And who might already be in there waiting?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What's striking about this story is how clearly it shows that recovery doesn't promise the absence of difficulty — it promises a different relationship with it. Three years sober and she still had the fear. The program didn't take the fear away. What it did was teach her how to walk *toward* the thing she was afraid of, with help, while staying open to whatever was on the other side of the door.

There's a phrase from contemplative traditions worth holding alongside this: the obstacle is the path. The thing you've been avoiding isn't blocking your growth — it is your growth, the moment you walk into it willingly. The elevator was the practice. The crying woman was the gift on the other side. And the gift, importantly, wasn't the absence of fear. It was the discovery that fear stops mattering as much when something bigger asks for your attention. The way out of the small self isn't a smaller fear. It's a wider room.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Step 7 lands today in the 12&12 — and Bill writes one of the most honest lines in the entire book: "we cling with a passionate persistence to others which are just as disturbing to our equilibrium, because we still enjoy them too much." Translation: some of your defects you actually like. The honesty of admitting that is the start of being able to ask for them to be removed.
  • Henrietta D. and AA member number three. Walk in Dry Places today: the wife of AA's third-ever member kept hoping her husband would recover when nobody else did. Bill W. and Dr. Bob showed up at the hospital. He left sober. He never drank again. The thing that kept him alive long enough to meet them was someone else's hope. If you're sitting with someone who hasn't gotten there yet — your hope counts. It's part of the math.
  • "Women Suffer, Too" (Big Book p. 203). A pioneering early member writes about going abroad, having her own business, indulging every desire — "and I was increasingly miserable." The unglamorous truth at the bottom of the privileged version of the disease: getting everything you want is not what fixes you.
  • Touchstones on the spaciousness of waiting. Brenda Ueland: inspiration "comes into us slowly and quietly and all the time, though we must regularly and every day give it a little chance to start flowing, prime it with a little solitude and idleness." If your week has been packed and your spiritual life feels dry, that's not a coincidence. The well refills in stillness, not in motion.
