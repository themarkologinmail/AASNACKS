📖 Today's Featured Reading

More Language of Letting Go — "Say When It's Time for Plan B" — May 15

"Say When It's Time for Plan B"

It was time to execute plan B. Whoosh. What a sweet sound that was, as the reserve canopy opened over my head. — Melody Beattie, More Language of Letting Go

I exited the plane, enjoyed my free fall, then checked my altimeter. Pull time. I deployed my parachute, waiting for that sweet whooshing sound, the one that meant I had a working canopy open. I didn't hear the sound. I was leaning backwards and turning, instead of floating softly toward the ground. I didn't have to do my eight-point canopy check. I knew immediately that something was wrong.

Ever since I had begun skydiving, I had been aware that although things mostly go well, sometimes they don't. For a while, I dreaded the possibility that something wouldn't be right with my canopy on opening, that I might have to cut it away. To deal with the fear and dread, I planned on having to use plan B — cutting away my main and pulling my reserve — each time I jumped out of the plane.

It's okay to have a plan. But take the time to develop a plan B, too. Know what you're going to do if plan A doesn't work out. Sometimes it's easier to come up with an option or an emergency procedure if we think it through before the crisis occurs. Then we don't have to panic. We can just institute the plan we rehearsed.

— Melody Beattie, More Language of Letting Go, May 15 entry. Beattie is the author of Codependent No More; this is the follow-up to her bestselling daily meditation book.

🎯 Deep Dive — The Parachute You Pack on a Good Day

Notice the timing in what Beattie's describing. She doesn't pack plan B mid-fall. She doesn't pack it on the plane. She doesn't pack it the morning of the jump. She packs it weeks before, on the ground, in a packing facility, in good light, with both hands free and her heart rate at sixty. The actual emergency takes maybe four seconds. The work that saved her took hours, on a day when nothing was wrong.

That's the move recovery is trying to teach. The first time you find out whether you have a sponsor's phone number memorized is not the first night you want to drink. The first time you find out whether you can identify a HALT trigger is not when you're already three of the four letters deep. The first time you find out whether you'll go to a meeting at 9 p.m. on a Tuesday is not at 8:55 p.m. on a Tuesday. The plan you can actually execute under pressure is the plan you built when there was no pressure.

This is, quietly, what most of the program is. The Steps aren't a crisis response. They're the packing. You inventory now so that when a resentment fires up on a Thursday afternoon you already know the procedure. You make amends now so the past stops generating fresh shame that you'd have to numb later. You build the meeting habit on the easy days so it holds on the hard ones. Every spiritual practice in AA — prayer, gratitude lists, the daily Tenth-Step check — is a reserve canopy you're folding in advance.

The other half of Beattie's piece is the part most people skip: she had been rehearsing the emergency every single jump. Not just thinking about it. Mentally walking it. "If the main fails, my left hand goes here, my right hand goes here, this is what I look at, this is how I land." So when it actually happened, the part of her brain that panics never got a vote. The rehearsed pattern fired before the fear did.

Worth asking yourself: what's your plan B? Not in theory. Not in a list of slogans. Specifically: if you wake up tomorrow morning and your head is already telling you a drink would feel good — what do you do in the first thirty seconds? What do you do in the first ten minutes? Who do you call before you call your dealer or your bartender? Where do you go before you go somewhere worse? If you don't know, you haven't packed the reserve yet. The good news: today, on a perfectly ordinary Thursday, is the right day to pack it.

What's the procedure you'd want already loaded if today turned out to be the hard day?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's a quiet kindness in how Beattie frames this, and it's worth sitting with. She doesn't moralize the malfunction. She doesn't say you should have packed better, you should have been more careful, you should have known. Her main canopy failed — and her piece is generous toward that fact. Things go wrong. Sometimes the equipment doesn't work. Sometimes the relationship doesn't work. Sometimes the plan doesn't work. None of that is a referendum on whether you deserved a working canopy.

What recovery adds is something even gentler: you can build the reserve while the main is still working. Most of us think of preparation as something we do once we know there's a problem. The wisdom of this piece runs the other way. The healthiest version of any of us is the one who's already folding the next parachute on the day there's nothing to fear — not because we expect to fall, but because, having fallen once, we know the work of falling is best done on the ground, well in advance, with steady hands.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Step 11 lands again in the 12&12 — and Bill's warning is sharp: there's a particular kind of AA, he writes, "who tries to run his life rigidly by this kind of prayer, by this self-serving demand of God for replies." The danger isn't asking God for guidance; the danger is mistaking your own wishful thinking for it. A useful follow-up to May 13's "if it be Thy will" qualifier.
  • A Day at a Time on the loneliness in early sobriety — "Even when I was surrounded by people, including my own family, the sense of 'aloneness' was overwhelming." The line that lands: the mystery of being alone in a crowd. Anyone who got sober knows that one.
  • Touchstones for Men — "If you can't fight and you can't flee, flow." A quiet correction to the win-everything posture most men show up with. "We can breathe a sigh of relief because much of the tension in our lives is reduced when we stop trying to conquer every moment and instead simply flow with it."
  • The Language of Letting Go on healthy risk — Beattie back-to-back today. "The goal of recovery is not to live life perfectly. The goal of recovery is to live, learn our lessons, and make overall progress." A useful sentence to keep nearby.
